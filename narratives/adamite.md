## blurb

Zn₂(AsO₄)(OH) — orthorhombic zinc arsenate hydroxide, the Zn end of the olivenite-adamite series. Forms in supergene oxidation zones where Zn-bearing sulfides (sphalerite) and arsenic-bearing fluids meet near-neutral pH. Yellow-green fluorescence under shortwave UV is the diagnostic — copper traces substituting for zinc activate the glow.

## variant: cuproadamite

This crystal contains copper substituting for zinc — enough Cu²⁺ to activate brilliant green fluorescence under UV light. The cuproadamite variety: the same mineral, but one that carries light inside it. Under short-wave UV it glows apple-green.

## variant: classic_yellow_green

Yellow-green adamite — the classic habit of Ojuela Mine, Mexico. No copper in this one; under UV the low-Cu zones fluoresce intensely while heavy-Cu zones stay dark. The contradiction crystal — identical composition except for the trace element that makes all the difference.

## variant: on_iron_oxide

Growing on iron oxide — the classic association. Limonite and goethite are the matrix, the rusty scaffolding left behind when primary sulfides oxidize. Adamite grows in the gaps, the secondary crystal decorating the ruins of earlier chemistry.

## variant: acicular_sprays

Fan-shaped sprays of acicular crystals radiating from a point. Each needle is a single crystal, but together they form a structure that looks like a frozen firework.

## variant: acid_dissolution

Acid dissolved the adamite — released Zn²⁺ and arsenate back to the fluid, potentially feeding later mimetite or olivenite growth.

## variant: olivenite_companion

Olivenite companion — there's an active olivenite (#{olivenite_id}) in this vug. Adamite and olivenite are end-members of the same arsenate structure: Zn₂AsO₄(OH) here, Cu₂AsO₄(OH) there. The Cu/Zn ratio in the supergene fluid decides which one crystallizes from any given pocket. Tsumeb shows both on the same matrix when the local Cu/Zn ratio fluctuates between fluid pulses.

## variant: collectors_tail

Adamite is a collector's mineral — not a building block of the crust, but a rare secondary phase that forms only when zinc, arsenic, and oxygen meet in the oxidation zone of a very specific kind of deposit. Every adamite specimen is evidence that something dramatic happened here first.
